LEARNING TO RESCALE: ON-THE-FLY SEQUENCE LENGTH ADAPTATION IN NON-AUTOREGRESSIVE SPEECH SYNTHESIS

Jiawei Jin1,∗, Ren Wang1,∗, Zhiyu Cui2, Shun Lei1, Yixuan Zhou1, Zhiyong Wu1,†

1Shenzhen International Graduate School, Tsinghua University, China
2Ant Group, China
∗Equal contribution, †Corresponding author

Abstract: Non-autoregressive (NAR) text-to-speech (TTS) models have gained significant attention for their parallel inference and global style consistency. However, their reliance on accurate character-level or global duration predictions remains a critical bottleneck, limiting both synthesis fidelity and naturalness. In this paper, we propose ElasticDLM (Elastic-length Diffusion Language Model), a novel variable-length NAR TTS model that eliminates the strong dependency on accurate duration predictions. Specifically, we introduce two functional tokens, along with a Differentiated Length-Scaling Training Scheme (DLTS) and a Hierarchical Confidence-Guided Inference (HCGI) strategy tailored for speech characteristics, enabling the model to learn length-scaling features and automatically adjust sequence length during inference. Experiments show that ElasticDLM produces high-fidelity, natural speech from arbitrary-length inputs, demonstrating a more flexible and simplified approach to high-quality speech synthesis.

Main Results

We conducted our evaluations on the seed-test-zh and seed-test-en benchmarks from Seed-TTS, using MaskGCT as our primary baseline. We designed three distinct settings for the MaskGCT baseline. The first, termed Normal, represents the standard MaskGCT which relies on its built-in duration model. The second, Fix, simulates a poor duration estimate by initializing the sequence to a fixed length (4s for Chinese, 4.5s for English). Finally, to serve as a performance upper bound, the GT setting provides MaskGCT with the ground-truth audio length.

Different Initial Length

To validate the powerful length adaptation capability of our model, this section tests its speech generation performance under conditions of varying initial sequence lengths. This experiment demonstrates that the model does not rely on precise prior length information and can robustly generate high-quality target speech from varied initial states, highlighting its flexibility and robustness.

Analysis of Application Flexibility

Fig. 2: The AB Test on Highly Expressive Single-speaker Dataset.

To assess application flexibility, we fine-tuned our model and the MaskGCT baseline on a highly expressive single-speaker dataset. An AB preference test was then conducted, where participants evaluated speech synthesized from held-out texts in four emotional styles: Fearful, Sad, Coquettish, and Excited.
